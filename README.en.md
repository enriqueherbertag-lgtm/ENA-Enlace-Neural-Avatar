The most famous brain-machine interfaces require surgery. Implants, brain electrodes, infection risks, rejection, and costly procedures. That limits them to laboratories or extreme cases.

**ENA was created to change that.**

## What it is

ENA (Electro Neural Adapter) is a non-invasive, modular, open-source brain-machine interface. It is placed behind the ear (retroauricular region) and operates without surgery, using dry electrodes to read EEG signals and tACS stimulation to send sensations.

## What it does

- **Reads brain signals**: 8–16 EEG channels capture neuronal activity from the scalp surface.
- **Writes sensations**: tACS stimulation (0–250 Hz, up to 2 mA) delivers touch, pressure, or temperature to the user.
- **Detects movement**: IMU (accelerometer + gyroscope) to interpret motor intention.
- **Connects wirelessly**: Bluetooth 5.2, Wi-Fi, and USB-C to communicate with prosthetics, CORPUS, or other devices.
- **Runs all day**: rechargeable battery with 8–12 hours of continuous use.

All in a device weighing less than 50 grams.

## Who it is for

- **Prosthetics users**: can feel what they touch and control the robotic hand with their thoughts.
- **CORPUS users**: can operate the artificial body remotely and receive sensory feedback.
- **Neurological rehabilitation**: stimulation for patients with motor damage.
- **Brain-machine interface research**: open platform for developing new applications.

## Main advantages

- **No surgery**: worn like a hearing aid, no risks or invasive procedures.
- **Bidirectional**: not only reads the brain, but also writes sensations.
- **Lightweight and portable**: less than 50 grams, 8–12 hours of battery life.
- **Integrated with CORPUS**: allows a human to control the artificial body and feel what it feels.
